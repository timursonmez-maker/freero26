The Four German Cases

Every noun or pronoun uses one of four cases depending on its role. Certain prepositions and verbs force a specific case.

Nominative

Wer? / Was?

The subject. Also for predicate nouns after sein, bleiben, werden.

Der Junge gab dem Hund einen Knochen.

Accusative

Wen? / Was?

The direct object. Also after accusative prepositions.

Der Junge gab dem Hund einen Knochen.

Dative

Wem?

The indirect object. Also after dative prepositions and verbs.

Der Junge gab dem Hund einen Knochen.

Genitive

Wessen?

Marks the possessor. Also after genitive prepositions.

Der Hund des Nachbarn.

IX
Adverbs

Adverbs answer when?, how?, where? — generally in that order.

Time

gestern (yesterday), heute (today), morgen (tomorrow), jetzt (now), bald (soon), sofort (immediately), früh (early), spät (late)

Manner

allein (alone), zusammen (together), leider (unfortunately), sehr (very), beinahe (almost)

Place

hier (here), da (there), überall (everywhere), oben (up), unten (down), weg (away)

Conjunctive adverbs connect ideas and invert subject/verb: außerdem, jedoch, trotzdem, deshalb, dennoch, also, sonst.

Word Order
  • 1.Statements: Lead verb always in Position 2. Heute gehe ich in die Schule.
  • 2.Multiple verbs: Past participles and infinitives go to the end. Ich muss morgen arbeiten.
  • 3.Questions: Lead verb moves to Position 1. Lernst du heute Deutsch?
  • 4.Dependent clauses: Subordinating conjunctions and relative pronouns send the lead verb to the end.
  • 5.Object order: Indirect before direct — unless direct is a pronoun: Ich gab es ihm.

  • 1.Subject pronouns often omitted — verb endings show person: Hablo español.

What Makes Turkish Different

Turkish is an agglutinative language — meaning is built by stacking suffixes onto root words. A single Turkish word can express what English needs an entire sentence for.

Key features: (1) No grammatical gender — no masculine/feminine/neuter distinction at all. (2) Vowel harmony governs every suffix — front/back and rounded/unrounded. (3) SOV word order — Subject, Object, Verb (verb comes last). (4) No articles in the European sense — definiteness is shown through word order and case suffixes. (5) Postpositions instead of prepositions (they come after the noun). (6) No irregular verbs — only 13 verbs have minor irregularities. Everything follows rules.
Evlerinizden = ev + ler + iniz + den
house + plural + your (formal) + from = "from your houses"

Definite vs. Indefinite

Turkish has no articles like "the" or "a." Instead:

Indefinite (a book)Use bir or bare noun: Bir kitap okudum (I read a book) / Kitap okudum (I read books)
Vowel Harmony — The Master Rule

Every suffix in Turkish must harmonise with the vowels of the root word. There are two types of harmony.

Two-Way Harmony (e/a)

The suffix vowel is e or a based on the last vowel of the root:

Last vowel is...Suffix usesExample
e, i, ö, ü (front)eev → evler, gül → güller
a, ı, o, u (back)akapı → kapılar, yol → yollar

Used by: plural (-lar/-ler), past tense (-dı/-di/-du/-dü and -tı/-ti), locative (-da/-de), ablative (-dan/-den).

Four-Way Harmony (ı/i/u/ü)

The suffix vowel is one of four options based on backness AND rounding:

Last vowelSuffix usesExample
a, ı (back unrounded)ıkapı → kapının
e, i (front unrounded)iev → evimin
o, u (back rounded)uyol → yolun
ö, ü (front rounded)ügöz → gözün

Used by: genitive (-(n)ın/in/un/ün), accusative (-(y)ı/i/u/ü), possessive suffixes, and many verb suffixes.

Consonant mutation: After voiceless consonants (ç, f, h, k, p, s, ş, t), suffixes starting with d become t, and c becomes ç. Also, final k often softens to ğ before a vowel suffix: kitap → kitabı, renk → rengi.

Negation & Question

Negative: Insert -ma/-me (or -mı/-mi for aorist) after the stem, before the tense suffix: gelmedim (I didn't come).

Key Verb: olmak (to be / to become)

Turkish has no direct equivalent of "to be" in the present tense — it's expressed with suffixes on nouns/adjectives:

Var / yok: Used for existence: Süt var (There is milk). Param yok (I have no money — lit. "my money doesn't exist").

Turkish often uses verbal suffixes instead of conjunctions for subordination — e.g. -dığı için (because...), -ınca (when...), -madan (without doing...). This is a major structural difference from European languages.

Word Order
Default: SOV — Subject + Object + Verb. The verb almost always comes last.
  • 1.Basic order: Subject + Time + Place + Object + Verb. Ben dün okulda Türkçe öğrendim (I yesterday at-school Turkish learned).
  • 2.Adjectives before nouns: güzel ev (beautiful house).
  • 3.Modifiers before the word they modify: adverbs before verbs, adjectives before nouns.
  • 4.Focus position: The word immediately before the verb gets the most emphasis. Move important information there.
  • 5.Question words go in the position of the word they replace: Ne yedin? (What did you eat?) — ne replaces the object, which normally comes before the verb.
  • 6.Flexible for emphasis: While SOV is default, word order can shift for emphasis or contrast. The verb still prefers the final position.

Turkish builds complex meanings by chaining suffixes in a fixed order. Understanding this order is the key to fluency.
